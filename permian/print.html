Permian Period Exploration

Essential Question & Claim: Why is the Permian period significant in Earth's history?

Analysis

Detail 1: The Permian period was the last of the Paleozoic Era, with the supercontinent Pangaea forming.
(Pangaea: /pænˈdʒiː.ə/)
Detail 2: It featured diverse marine life, including brachiopods and ammonites.
(Brachiopods: /ˈbræk.i.oʊˌpɒdz/)
Detail 3: Significant advances in reptiles led to the dominance of therapsids.
(Therapsids: /θəˈræp.sɪdz/)
Detail 4: It ended with the largest mass extinction, wiping out around 90% of marine species.
(Extinction: /ɪkˈstɪŋk.ʃən/)
Detail 5: This period paved the way for the rise of dinosaurs in the Mesozoic Era.
(Mesozoic: /ˌmɛz.oʊˈzoʊ.ɪk/)
Guadalupe Mountains: Texas's Guadalupe Mountains hold one of the largest exposed fossil reefs from the Permian period. A tropical ocean once covered the region, leaving a 400-mile-long reef that preserved thousands of marine fossils. Photograph by Jonathan Blair/Corbis
Trilobite Fossil: Trilobites thrived for millions of years before being wiped out in the Permian extinction. They are among the most successful organisms in Earth's history. Photograph by James L. Amos

Conclusion

The Permian period was crucial in Earth's history, marking significant geological and biological changes that set the stage for future evolution.
